她不像黛玉那樣總是讓他心痛，也不像寶釵那樣端莊穩重，使他敬而遠之， 她更像一個可愛的大姐姐， 更像是一種迷人的粉紅色朦朧迷夢， 讓寶玉希望永遠置身其中， 不出來。而偏偏，這場夢，很快就碎了。 秦可卿的死，像一團黑色迷霧，從頭到尾，都透著說不出的古怪與陰森。 表面上，曹雪芹寫她是病死。就是現在我們看到的，那個一百二十回的「程甲本」紅樓夢， 所描述的。 可是越細讀，越深思， 越讓人感到，事情絕不那麼簡單。尤其是《脂硯齋》留下的那句著名批語： 「秦可卿淫喪天香樓。」 這裡要稍微解釋一下， 誰是脂硯齋， 脂硯齋留下的那句著名批語， 「秦可卿淫喪天香樓。」又是怎麼回事？ 脂硯齋，是一個人名，是《紅樓夢》前八十回原稿的讀者與點批者，她與曹雪芹關係極為親密， 有人說她其實就是曹雪芹的妻子、也有人說是曹雪芹的親友、更有人認為，那其實就是曹雪芹本人。 不管怎麼說， 脂硯齋， 夫人也罷， 親友也罷， 甚至是曹雪芹本人。 這個當時的小說原稿讀者， 可以在曹雪芹原著書稿上做點批， 應該是最了解曹雪芹的人， 所以由她批注過的紅樓夢原稿， 應該更加符合曹雪芹當時的創作構想 。 由脂硯齋評註過的《石頭記》， 石頭記是紅樓夢的原名，被後人稱為《脂本》。 脂本實際就是曹雪芹的原創手稿的手抄本 ， 所以在脂本中的批注：「秦可卿淫喪天香樓。」， 說明曹雪芹原來寫秦可卿之死， 並不是病死， 而是《淫喪天香樓》。 是因為淫亂， 死於天香樓。 那麼問題來了， 秦可卿在天香樓， 和誰淫亂？ 為什麼會喪命呢 ？ 對於這個問題， 當然會引起紅學專家們的重視， 許多紅樓夢研究學者考證， 得出結論， 曹雪芹的原著， 秦可卿不是病死的， 而是因為和公公賈珍發生不倫關係， 就是所謂的爬灰， 事情敗露，被迫在天香樓上吊身亡。 可以想像一下， 秦可卿， 一個沒有任何家庭背景， 指責秦可卿風流成性， 主動勾引賈珍， 事發後被迫上吊身亡。 筆者不是紅學專家， 不知道這些紅學專家， 為什麼一定要把髒水潑到秦可卿身上。 這件事，還需要考證嗎？ 一邊是掌握家族生殺大權的大家長賈珍，一邊是年紀輕輕， 毫無背景的兒媳婦， 年輕貌美卻柔弱無助的秦可卿， 那麼， 這種不倫關係的發生， 誰是始作俑者， 還有考證必要嗎 ？ 中國男人， 特別是權高位重者， 歷來有個惡習， 自己做錯了事， For Baoyu, there was something even more special about Qin Keqing: his awakening to desire, his very first contact with the mysteries between men and women, took place in Qin Keqing’s chamber. That day, Baoyu accompanied Grandmother Jia to the Ning Mansion to admire the flowers. At noon he grew sleepy and took a nap in Qin Keqing’s room. In his dream he wandered into the Land of Illusion and first experienced the pleasures of love with the Fairy of Combined Beauty. And that fairy’s childhood name just happened to be “Keqing.” This was no coincidence. Cao Xueqin was hinting that Qin Keqing herself was the initiator of Baoyu’s youthful world of desire. Because of this, Qin Keqing held an extraordinarily special place in Baoyu’s heart. To Baoyu, Qin Keqing was different because she did not constantly make his heart ache the way Daiyu did, nor was she so dignified and proper as Baochai, causing him to keep his distance. She was more like a lovely older sister, more like a soft pink dream wrapped in mist, a dream Baoyu wished to remain inside forever. Yet that dream shattered very quickly. The death of Qin Keqing is like a cloud of fog. From beginning to end, it carries a strange and sinister air that cannot be explained away. On the surface, Cao Xueqin wrote that she died of illness. That is the version described in the one-hundred-and-twenty-chapter Chengjia edition of Dream of the Red Chamber that people read today. Yet the more carefully one reads, the more deeply one thinks, the more one feels that things were not so simple. Especially because of the famous annotation left behind by Zhiyanzhai: “Qin Keqing died from sexual scandal at the Tower of Heavenly Fragrance.” Here it is necessary to explain briefly who Zhiyanzhai was, and what this famous annotation actually means. Zhiyanzhai was the name of a person, the reader and commentator of the original first eighty chapters of Dream of the Red Chamber. This person was extremely close to Cao Xueqin. Some say Zhiyanzhai was actually Cao Xueqin’s wife. Others say it was a close relative or friend. Some even believe it was Cao Xueqin himself. Whatever the truth may be, whether Zhiyanzhai was a wife, a friend, or even Cao Xueqin himself, this reader of the original manuscript, who was able to write commentary directly onto Cao Xueqin’s handwritten drafts, must have been among the people who understood Cao Xueqin best. Therefore, the manuscript annotated by Zhiyanzhai should also be closer to Cao Xueqin’s original creative intentions. The annotated version of The Story of the Stone, which was the original title of Dream of the Red Chamber, later came to be known as the “Zhi edition.” The Zhi edition was essentially a handwritten copy of Cao Xueqin’s original manuscript. Therefore, the annotation found in the Zhi edition — “Qin Keqing died from sexual scandal at the Tower of Heavenly Fragrance” — indicates that in Cao Xueqin’s original conception, Qin Keqing did not die of illness at all. She died through “sexual scandal at the Tower of Heavenly Fragrance.” In other words, she died because of illicit sexual relations. Then the question naturally arises: with whom was Qin Keqing involved at the Tower of Heavenly Fragrance? And why did it cost her life? Naturally, such a question drew the attention of Redology scholars. Many researchers of Dream of the Red Chamber concluded that in Cao Xueqin’s original story, Qin Keqing did not die of illness. Rather, she engaged in an incestuous relationship with her father-in-law Jia Zhen — what was known as “climbing the ashes” — and after the affair was exposed, she was forced to hang herself in the Tower of Heavenly Fragrance. Imagine this clearly. Qin Keqing was a girl with no family background at all, an orphan adopted from an orphanage by the minor official Qin Ye. She was young and beautiful, and because of her beauty, Jia Zhen selected her and married her to his son Jia Rong. Because she was beautiful, and because she was weak and vulnerable, from the very beginning she was desired by the wolf-like and lustful Jia Zhen. Under the pretext of arranging a marriage for his son, he locked Qin Keqing inside the cage of the Ning Mansion, until at last the incestuous relationship occurred exactly as he wished. Jia Zhen suffered no consequences whatsoever afterward, yet Qin Keqing was supposedly the one who had to hang herself. The hidden truth behind this, anyone living today should be able to understand with even the slightest bit of thought. There are Redology scholars who have written long articles arguing that Qin Keqing herself was promiscuous by nature, that she deliberately seduced Jia Zhen, and that after the affair was exposed she was forced to hang herself. I am not a Redology scholar. I truly do not understand why these scholars insist on pouring all the blame onto Qin Keqing. Does this matter really still require investigation? On one side stood Jia Zhen, the patriarch who held absolute power of life and death within the family. On the other side stood Qin Keqing, a young daughter-in-law with no background whatsoever, beautiful yet helpless and defenseless. Under such circumstances, who was truly responsible for initiating this relationship? Is there really any need to “research” that question? Chinese men, especially those in positions of great power, have long possessed a terrible habit. When they commit wrongdoing, they refuse to accept responsibility themselves, and instead push the blame onto women, sanctimoniously calling them “disasters brought by beauty.” From Mo Xi of the Xia dynasty, to Daji of the Shang dynasty; from Bao Si of the Western Zhou, to Yang Guifei of the Tang dynasty — the fall of which dynasty was truly caused by these powerless women hidden away in palace chambers, rather than by foolish emperors themselves? I do not believe Cao Xueqin thought this way. He would never have accepted this theory of “fatal women” used to shift men’s responsibilities onto women. He would never have blamed the fall of the Jia family upon pitiable women such as Qin Keqing. Through Baoyu’s own words, Cao Xueqin clearly expressed his understanding of women: they were as pure as water itself. He would never have attributed Jia Zhen’s lust and depravity to the unfortunate Qin Keqing. If he could see today’s Redology scholars enthusiastically discussing theories about Qin Keqing being lustful and shamelessly seducing Jia Zhen, he would probably leap angrily from his grave to argue with them in person. After Qin Keqing died, the entire Jia household shook with cries of grief, especially Jia Zhen himself. His reaction far exceeded the sorrow a normal father-in-law would show toward a daughter-in-law. He wept as though mourning his own parents, almost out of his mind with grief. For Qin Keqing’s funeral he spared no expense, even using precious timber originally reserved for Prince Yizhong to construct her coffin. Qin Keqing’s funeral was conducted with earthshaking extravagance. Because of its excessive grandeur, its illegal coffin, and the attendance of guests whose rank violated court ritual — including the illegal appearance of the Prince of Beijing — the Rong and Ning Houses displayed arrogance and overreach before the imperial center of power. In the end, the emperor came to regard the Ning Mansion as the chief offender, setting the entire Jia clan upon an irreversible path toward ruin. But that comes later in the story. Does this not seem strange? Why would the death of a daughter-in-law warrant such an enormous spectacle? When the old stableman Jiao Da became drunk, he once shouted: “Those who crawl into ashes crawl into ashes, and those who sleep with younger brothers sleep with younger brothers!” The phrase “climbing the ashes” was a vulgar folk expression referring to a father-in-law violating his son’s wife. Cao Xueqin never stated it openly, yet through Jiao Da’s drunken words, he had already made it perfectly clear. From top to bottom, the entire Ning Mansion was filled with a rotten and licentious atmosphere. And trapped within it all, Qin Keqing, though outwardly glamorous, was very likely already collapsing inside, suffering beyond endurance. She was an orphan, a fragile young woman with no powerful family background and no authority of her own. Though she appeared to live among silks and luxuries inside a noble mansion, in truth she was terribly weak and defenseless. Before the violations of the patriarch Jia Zhen, she had no power to resist. If her father-in-law wanted to violate her, what could she possibly do? If she resisted, she would not survive. If she submitted, she still would not survive. In the end, Jia Zhen obtained what he wanted, while she alone was forced to die and bear the reputation of being sexually immoral. And to preserve the dignity of the patriarch Jia Zhen, the true cause of her death could never be revealed publicly. Thus, “death by illness” became the most respectable explanation. Yet Cao Xueqin was ultimately unwilling to accept that completely, so he left behind this annotation in the Zhi edition: “Qin Keqing died from sexual scandal at the Tower of Heavenly Fragrance.” That annotation is like a drop of black blood seeping into the most magnificent parts of Dream of the Red Chamber, forcing readers to think for themselves. And the young Baoyu, after Qin Keqing’s death, truly felt the terror of death for the first time. Before this, he had heard of people dying, but those deaths were far removed from him. Qin Keqing was different. She was someone who had genuinely cared for him. Someone who had truly shown him tenderness. She was the first person in Baoyu’s youth who made him vaguely understand the meaning of “love.” Yet such a wonderful person suddenly died. And died in such a mysterious, oppressive, unspeakable way. Though Baoyu was young, he was not foolish. He had already begun to sense vaguely that the words spoken by the adults were not the truth. Behind those luxurious courtyards, splendid clothes, fine food, laughter, and celebration, there lurked something profoundly dark and filthy. But at that time Baoyu was still too young. He did not yet truly understand that many of the young and beautiful girls inside the Grand View Garden already had their fates decided from the very beginning. Qin Keqing was only the first. （To be continued）
